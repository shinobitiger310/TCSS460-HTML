Narwhals

The “unicorns of the sea” — Monodon monoceros

Overview

Narwhals are medium-sized toothed whales that live in the Arctic waters of Canada, Greenland, and Russia. They’re best known for the males’ long, spiraled tusk — an elongated tooth that can grow up to ~3 meters.

They dive deeply (often over 1,000 meters) to hunt fish, squid, and shrimp, navigating sea ice with remarkable adaptations to cold and low light.

The Famous Tusk

The narwhal tusk is a sensory organ packed with nerve endings, likely helping the animal detect changes in water and prey. While most males have one tusk, rare individuals can have two. Females occasionally grow shorter tusks.

Habitat & Range

Narwhals spend most of their lives in cold Arctic waters. Seasonal sea-ice changes influence their migrations between coastal summering grounds and deeper offshore wintering areas.

Conservation

Narwhals are sensitive to climate-driven sea-ice loss and increasing human activity in the Arctic. Conservation focuses on sustainable harvests, monitoring populations, and protecting key habitats.

Quick Facts

  • Scientific name: Monodon monoceros
  • Length: ~3.9–5.5 m (males typically larger)
  • Tusk length: up to ~3 m (usually males)
  • Diet: Greenland halibut, Arctic cod, squid, shrimp
  • Range: Arctic Ocean (Canada, Greenland, Russia)
